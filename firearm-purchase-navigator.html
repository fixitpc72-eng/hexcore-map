What to Ask the Dealer Before the Transfer
What is your transfer fee for an online FFL transfer, and does it change for handguns vs long guns?
Do you accept the shipping format from the retailer I'm buying from (some dealers won't accept certain carriers or packaging)?
What ID and documentation do you require beyond a government photo ID — especially if my state has a permit, license, or certificate requirement?
Is there anything on your end that could affect the timing of this transfer — state waiting periods, local paperwork, or delays in your process?
What happens if my NICS check is delayed — will you hold the firearm, and for how long?
Do you provide a receipt for the transfer and a copy of the completed Form 4473 for my records?
Is there a separate fee for any required safe-storage equipment or trigger lock if your state or locality requires it?
Document Checklist — Bring to the Dealer
Government-issued photo ID: Driver's license, state ID, or passport — must be current and show your current address
Secondary residence document (if your ID address is outdated): utility bill, bank statement, or lease showing your current address
State-required permit, license, or certificate if applicable in your state (check your state result above)
Payment method the dealer accepts for the transfer fee, state fee, and any other charges
Tracking number for any online purchase being received by the FFL
Order confirmation or invoice from the online retailer for your records

The Background Check Process
ATF Form 4473
Every dealer sale starts here. You fill out this federal form — full legal name, date of birth, address, citizenship, and eligibility questions. Answer every question accurately. A false statement on Form 4473 is a federal felony regardless of whether the transaction is approved.

The dealer sends your info to NICS or your state POC system. You get a response: Proceed, Delay, or Deny.
↗ ATF Form 4473 (PDF)
FBI NICS vs State POC
Some states run their own background check systems (Point of Contact states) rather than going directly to the FBI. In POC states, the dealer contacts your state agency instead of FBI NICS directly.

POC states: CA, CO, CT, FL, HI, IL, NJ, NV, OR, PA, TN, TX, UT, VA, WA, WI, plus DC. Partial POC: AZ, MD, MI, NE, NH, NM, NY, NC, ND, SC.

Your rights after a denial are the same regardless of which system processed your check.

Understanding Your NICS Response
Proceed
The background check cleared. The dealer may transfer the firearm to you. This is the normal outcome for eligible buyers. A Proceed means no prohibiting records were found in the checked databases at this time.
Delay
Not a denial. NICS found a record that requires more research before an approval or denial can be issued. The dealer cannot transfer the firearm during this review window.

Federal law gives the FBI 3 business days to resolve a delay. If not resolved in 3 business days, the dealer may (but is not required to) transfer the firearm. State law can require dealers to hold longer regardless.
Deny
The system found a record that prohibits the transfer. The dealer cannot transfer the firearm.

A denial is not always final — NICS denials are sometimes wrong. You have the right to appeal through the FBI NICS Voluntary Appeal File (VAF) process. See Tab 03 for the full appeal process.
Common Causes of Delay
Name or DOB Match
Your name or date of birth closely matches a record in the system. This does not mean you are prohibited — NICS needs time to verify you are not the person in the record.
Incomplete Record
A state has submitted a prohibiting record but not all required details. NICS needs to contact the state for clarification before issuing a decision.
Disposition Missing
An arrest record exists but no final court disposition is on file. Without knowing the outcome of the arrest, NICS cannot determine if a prohibition applies.
Mental Health Records
Involuntary commitment records or adjudication records are sometimes incomplete or inaccurate across state systems. A match here requires manual review.
Immigration Status
Non-citizen status records require additional verification. Certain visa categories prohibit firearm purchase; others do not. NICS must verify which applies.
System Volume
High-volume periods (holidays, political events) can slow NICS response times. A delay during these periods may simply reflect system load rather than a record issue.
If You Are Delayed
1
Get Your NTN (NICS Transaction Number)
The dealer receives a NICS Transaction Number when the delay is issued. Write this number down. You will need it to follow up on the status of your check and to file an appeal if the delay results in a denial.
2
Wait Out the 3-Business-Day Window
Federal law gives NICS 3 business days to resolve a delay. After 3 business days, the dealer may transfer the firearm. However, state law may require a longer hold — in some states, the dealer must wait for a final Proceed before transferring.
States with mandatory extended holds include California, Colorado, Florida, Illinois, Maryland, New Jersey, and others. Ask your dealer about your state's requirement.
3
Follow Up with FBI NICS Directly
You can contact the FBI NICS Section directly to check on the status of a delayed transaction. Have your NTN and personal information ready.

FBI NICS: 1-877-444-6427  |  FBI NICS for buyers ↗
4
If Delay Converts to Denial, Move to the Appeal Process
If the delay resolves as a denial, you have the right to appeal. See Tab 03 for the full denial appeal process, including the VAF and UPIN program.
⚠ A denial is not always the final answer. NICS error rates are real. Mismatched records, incomplete dispositions, and outdated information are documented causes of wrongful denials. The appeal process exists specifically to correct these errors.
What VAF and UPIN Mean
VAF — Voluntary Appeal File
The VAF is a program run by the FBI NICS Section that allows individuals who have been incorrectly denied or repeatedly delayed to submit documentation proving their eligibility. Once approved, your records are updated so future checks process faster and more accurately.
UPIN — Unique Personal Identification Number After your VAF appeal is approved, the FBI issues you a UPIN — a personal number you give to the dealer at the time of each purchase. The dealer includes your UPIN on the Form 4473, and NICS uses it to immediately pull your pre-verified record, dramatically reducing delay and denial risk for all future purchases.
Common Reasons for Wrongful Denial
Name / DOB Mismatch
Your name, date of birth, or other identifiers closely match a prohibited person's record in the system. You are not that person. The record needs to be distinguished from your file.
Expunged or Sealed Record
A prior conviction was expunged or sealed, but the prohibition record was never removed from NICS databases. The expungement restored your rights — the system just hasn't caught up.
Outdated Mental Health Record
An old involuntary commitment or adjudication that no longer applies, was overturned, or was incorrectly entered is still appearing in the system.
Domestic Violence Record Error
A misdemeanor domestic violence record was entered incorrectly, the conviction was vacated, or the record doesn't meet the federal Lautenberg definition but is still flagging.
Felony Dismissed or Reduced
A felony charge was dismissed or reduced to a misdemeanor, but the original record remains without the final disposition attached, triggering the denial.
Drug Conviction Error
A drug conviction record is in the system but you believe it doesn't apply — either because of jurisdiction, timing, or an error in what was submitted to the federal database.
Step-by-Step Appeal Process
1
Get Your NTN from the Dealer
The NICS Transaction Number is issued when the denial occurs. The dealer receives it — ask them for it immediately. You have 30 days from the denial date to initiate an appeal. Don't wait.
2
Request the Reason for Denial
Contact the FBI NICS directly to request the reason for your denial. They are required to tell you the type of record that caused the denial (though not every specific detail). This tells you what documentation to gather.

FBI NICS: 1-877-444-6427  |  FBI NICS for buyers ↗
3
Submit a Challenge via the VAF Program
File a formal challenge with the FBI NICS Voluntary Appeal File. You submit documentation supporting your eligibility. The NICS Section reviews your records and responds with a decision or requests additional information.
FBI VAF mailing address: FBI NICS Section, Attn: Voluntary Appeal File, P.O. Box 4278, Clarksburg, WV 26302-4278. Online appeal portal available at the FBI link below.
↗ FBI NICS Appeal & VAF Information
4
If the Error Is in a State Record — Contact the State Agency
If FBI NICS tells you the denial is based on a state record, the FBI may direct you to the state agency that submitted the record. The state must update or correct its own records — the FBI cannot change state-submitted data. You will need to work with the state court or agency directly to get the record corrected or the disposition updated.
5
Apply for Your UPIN After VAF Approval
Once your VAF appeal is approved, the FBI issues you a Unique Personal Identification Number (UPIN). Provide this number to the dealer at each future purchase — include it on Form 4473. NICS uses it to pull your pre-verified file, which prevents future delays and denials caused by the same record issue.
6
If Appeals Fail — Consult a Firearms Attorney
If you believe you are eligible but the appeal process has not resolved the issue, consult a licensed firearms attorney. In some cases, federal court challenge of a wrongful denial is the next step. An attorney can also help navigate expungement, record sealing, restoration of rights, and other relief options in your state.
GOA legal resources and state-specific firearms attorneys can assist with appeals beyond the FBI level. See gunowners.org for resources.
Appeal Document Checklist
NTN written down: NICS Transaction Number from the dealer at time of denial — required to initiate an appeal
Denial reason requested: Called FBI NICS (1-877-444-6427) to get the type of record causing the denial
Personal ID documentation: Copy of government-issued photo ID showing full legal name, date of birth, and current address
Court disposition records: Final disposition documents for any prior arrest or charge referenced in the denial — dismissal, acquittal, reduced charge, or expungement paperwork
Expungement / sealing order: If your record was expunged or sealed, a certified copy of the court order restoring your rights
Mental health discharge or correction documentation: If the denial involves a mental health record, documentation that the commitment was voluntary, was overturned, or the adjudication was incorrect
Military discharge records: DD-214 or other discharge documentation if military service is part of your eligibility argument
VAF appeal form submitted: Formal challenge submitted to FBI NICS Voluntary Appeal File with all supporting documentation
State record correction initiated (if applicable): Contacted the state agency or court that submitted the record and started the correction or update process
Copies kept of everything submitted: Copies of all documents sent to FBI NICS and any state agencies, with dates and confirmation if available
