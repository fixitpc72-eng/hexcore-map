What Is CCW Reciprocity?

Understanding how your concealed carry permit works when you cross state lines — and where the system breaks down.

📖 Reciprocity Basics 🗽 Constitutional Carry 🃏 Multi-Permit Strategy 🚗 Vehicle Carry ✈️ Flying with Firearms 🏕️ National Parks
🤝
Full Reciprocity
The destination state fully honors your home state permit with no additional requirements. You carry under their state laws, but your permit is recognized as valid. Most common relationship between permit-friendly states.
⚠️
Partial / Restricted
The state honors permits from specific states only, sometimes with conditions like age requirements, training equivalency, or residency restrictions. Research the specific conditions before carrying.
🚫
No Reciprocity
The state does not honor any out-of-state permits. CA, NY, NJ, IL, HI, MD, MA are the primary examples. Do not carry without a state-specific permit in these states.
🗽
Constitutional Carry
No permit needed for eligible law-abiding citizens. 29 states currently have constitutional carry laws. You can carry without your home permit, but must still follow that state's carry laws.
⚠ Critical Rule: When carrying in another state, you must follow their laws — not your home state's laws. This includes magazine capacity limits, prohibited locations, duty-to-inform requirements, and signage rules. Ignorance of local law is not a legal defense.

Types of CCW Permits Explained

Shall-Issue

  • Must issue permit if applicant meets requirements
  • Requirements typically: background check, training, age, residency
  • Issuing authority has no discretion to deny qualified applicants
  • ~40 states are shall-issue
  • Most widely recognized permit type

May-Issue

  • Issuing authority may deny even qualified applicants
  • "Good cause" or "justifiable need" often required
  • CA, NY, MA, MD, HI, NJ are examples
  • Post-Bruen (2022) SCOTUS ruling, many May-Issue restrictions ruled unconstitutional
  • Laws in flux — verify current status

States That Honor No Out-of-State Permits

⛔ These 9 jurisdictions do not honor any out-of-state CCW permits: California, Connecticut, Hawaii, Illinois, Maryland, Massachusetts, New Jersey, New York, and the District of Columbia. Do not carry in these states/jurisdictions without their specific state permit.
California Connecticut Hawaii Illinois Maryland Massachusetts New Jersey New York District of Columbia
📌 Special Note — NY State vs. New York City: New York State permits are valid throughout the state — except New York City. NYC has its own separate licensing system with an extremely low approval rate for civilians. A NY State permit is NOT valid in the five boroughs.
No Permit Needed

Constitutional Carry Explained

29 states now allow permitless concealed carry. Here's what that means, what it doesn't mean, and why you still want a permit.

✅
What It Means
Any person legally allowed to possess a firearm can carry it concealed without obtaining a permit. No training, no application, no fee required for carry within the state.
❌
What It Doesn't Mean
Constitutional carry does not mean anything-goes. Prohibited locations still exist (schools, government buildings, private property with signs). Prohibited persons still cannot carry.
🃏
Still Get a Permit
Even in constitutional carry states, getting a permit is strongly recommended for interstate travel. Permits provide reciprocity in other states that won't honor constitutional carry alone.

The 29 Constitutional Carry States (as of 2026)

Alabama (2023) Alaska (2003) Arizona (2010) Arkansas (2023) Florida (2023) Georgia (2022) Idaho (2016) Indiana (2022) Iowa (2021) Kansas (2015) Kentucky (2019) Louisiana (2024) Maine (2015) Mississippi (2016) Missouri (2017) Montana (2021) Nebraska (2023) New Hampshire (2017) North Carolina (2023) Ohio (2022) Oklahoma (2019) South Carolina (2024) South Dakota (2019) Tennessee (2021) Texas (2021) Utah (2021) Vermont (since 1791) West Virginia (2016) Wyoming (2011)
🗽 Vermont: The original constitutional carry state — Vermont has allowed permitless carry since its founding in 1791. Because Vermont doesn't issue permits, Vermont residents should obtain non-resident permits (FL, UT, AZ) for interstate carry coverage.

Constitutional Carry vs. Permits

  • Constitutional carry states still often issue permits for reciprocity purposes
  • In most CC states, you can carry without the permit within that state
  • A permit from a CC state is still valuable for travel to other states
  • Texas HB 1927 (2021) created permitless carry but the LTC remains the best reciprocity tool
  • Florida SB 264 (2023) — residents can carry without permit but non-resident permits still honored

Key Restrictions That Still Apply

  • Federal prohibited persons cannot carry regardless of state law
  • School zones — federal Gun-Free School Zones Act (GFSZA) applies everywhere
  • Federal buildings, courthouses, polling places
  • Private property with posted no-carry signs or verbal notice
  • Bars / alcohol — many CC states still restrict carry while intoxicated or in establishments serving alcohol
  • Age requirements — most CC states require 18 or 21+

On the Road

Carrying in Your Vehicle

Vehicle carry laws vary dramatically by state. Here's what you need to know before every road trip.

⛔ Most Dangerous Mistake: Assuming your home state vehicle carry rules apply everywhere you drive. They don't. Each state you enter has its own rules for where the firearm must be, whether it can be loaded, and what you must do if stopped by police.
✅
Permit Holders
In states that honor your permit, you can generally carry your firearm loaded and accessible in your vehicle (on your person or in a holster). Follow their specific rules for where it can be stored.
📦
No-Permit States
In states that don't honor your permit, your firearm must be unloaded, in a locked case, and inaccessible (typically trunk or locked storage). Ammunition may need to be stored separately.
🚔
Police Stops
Many states have duty to inform requirements — you must tell the officer you have a permit and are carrying when stopped. Some states only require disclosure if asked. Know the rule before you drive in.

FOPA — Federal Safe Passage for Interstate Transport

⚖️
What FOPA Provides
The Firearm Owners Protection Act (FOPA) of 1986 provides a federal "safe passage" provision. If transporting a firearm through a state where carrying would otherwise be illegal, you may do so without violating federal law — under strict conditions.
📋
FOPA Requirements
To invoke FOPA protection: (1) Legal to possess the firearm at your origin AND destination. (2) Firearm must be unloaded. (3) Firearm must be in a locked container not accessible from the passenger compartment (not the glove box). (4) You must be passing through — not stopping for an extended period.
⚠️
FOPA Limitations
FOPA is a federal defense, not a guarantee against arrest. NJ, NY, and MD in particular have aggressively prosecuted travelers invoking FOPA. You may still be arrested and forced to prove the defense in court. Attorney involvement is expensive. Avoid non-honor states entirely when possible.

Vehicle Carry Quick Reference

Scenario What to Do Risk Level
Driving through a permit-honoring state Carry per their rules. Check open carry / duty to inform status. Low
Driving through CA, IL, NY, NJ, MD Unloaded, locked case, in trunk. No stops except gas/food/sleep. High

Duty to Inform States

  • Michigan: Must inform immediately upon contact
  • Ohio: Must inform immediately upon contact
  • North Carolina: Must inform upon request
  • Texas: Must display permit if asked
  • Illinois: Must inform — but carries nothing
  • Always be polite, calm, and keep hands visible regardless of state law

States Where Vehicle Carry Gets Complex

  • California: No loaded firearm in vehicle without CCW. Handguns must be locked container, unloaded.
  • New York City: Extremely strict. Even FOPA transport risky.
  • New Jersey: Very aggressive prosecution. Unloaded, locked, trunk.
  • Maryland: Unloaded, locked, not accessible from passenger area.
  • Illinois: Unloaded in enclosed case. Chicago especially aggressive.
Air Travel

Flying With a Firearm

TSA rules, airline policies, and destination state laws — everything you need to fly armed legally and without hassle.

✅ The Short Version: You CAN legally fly with a firearm — it must be in your checked baggage, unloaded, in a hard-sided locked case, and declared at check-in. It's a well-established process that millions of Americans use annually.

Step-by-Step: Flying with a Firearm

1
Use a Hard-Sided, Locked Case
The firearm must be in a hard-sided container (pelican case, hard-shell gun case) with a TSA-approved lock — but NOT a TSA master-key lock. Use YOUR lock. TSA cannot access it without your presence. The container can be inside a larger bag.
2
Unload the Firearm
The firearm must be completely unloaded. No magazine in the firearm, no round in the chamber. Ammunition can travel in the same locked container, but must be in its original box or purpose-built ammo container. Loose ammo in the case is generally not allowed.
3
Declare at Check-In
Inform the airline agent at check-in: "I have an unloaded firearm to declare." They'll give you a declaration card to sign and place inside the case. This is required. Do NOT wait to be asked — always declare proactively. Attempting to sneak a firearm through is a federal offense.
4
Follow the Airline's Specific Rules
Airlines have additional rules beyond TSA. Delta requires the case to be checked directly (not inside another bag in some instances). Southwest allows firearms in checked bags with specific procedures. Some airlines ban certain firearm types. Always check your airline's current firearms policy before travel.
5
Know the Laws at Your Destination
Federal rules get your firearm to the destination — but state laws govern what you can do once you land. Flying into California, New York, New Jersey, or Illinois? Your CCW permit means nothing there. You must transport the firearm to your final destination per that state's laws (unloaded, locked, etc.).

TSA Rules Summary

  • Checked baggage only — never carry-on
  • Hard-sided, locked container
  • Unloaded firearm
  • Declare at airline check-in counter
  • Ammunition: original packaging or ammo case, same container OK
  • Magazines: can be in the case; must be unloaded
  • Fines up to $13,910 for undeclared firearms

Pro Tips for Flying Armed

  • Arrive 30 minutes earlier than usual — declaration adds time
  • Use a Pelican 1510 or similar — airline agents recognize them
  • Don't advertise: use a discreet outer bag around the gun case
  • Keep airline's firearm policy screenshot on your phone
  • International travel: each country has own rules — research extensively before flying internationally with firearms
  • Cruises: similar to flying; check cruise line policy
⚠ Airport Layovers: If your layover is in a state like NJ (Newark EWR) or NY (JFK/LGA), your checked firearm is still in that state's jurisdiction if you leave the airport or your connection is cancelled. There have been prosecutions of travelers with layover connections. Be aware of your routing when flying through strict states.
Outdoors & Federal Lands

National Parks & Federal Lands

The 2010 law changed everything. Here's the current legal framework for carrying in national parks, forests, BLM land, and other federal property.

🏕️
National Parks (Post-2010)
Since February 22, 2010, carrying a firearm in a national park is governed by the law of the state in which the park is located. If the state allows concealed carry and your permit is honored, you can carry in the park. Same rules as carrying anywhere else in that state.
🌲
National Forests & BLM Land
National forests and Bureau of Land Management (BLM) lands generally follow state law. Hunting regulations also apply. These are typically the most firearm-friendly federal lands — carry per state law where the land is located.
🏛️
Federal Buildings Inside Parks
Even in carry-friendly parks, federal facilities are prohibited. This includes visitor centers, ranger stations, permit offices, and any federal building. Firearms must be stored in your vehicle when entering these buildings.
⛔
Always Prohibited Federal Areas
Regardless of state law or permits, these are always off-limits: federal courthouses, post offices, VA hospitals, military bases, TSA security checkpoints, and any building owned/leased by the federal government for official use.

Special Cases

🏞️
National Recreation Areas & Monuments
Same rule as national parks — follow the state's laws. However, some NRAs and monuments straddle state lines. Know which state section you're in. Yellowstone (WY/MT/ID), for example, — carry laws follow whichever state section you're in at the time.
🦌
Hunting in Federal Lands
Hunting with firearms in national forests and BLM land is generally allowed per state hunting regulations. National parks have strict no-hunting rules (separate from carry rules). You can carry in most parks for self-defense, but you cannot hunt wildlife.
🏔️
National Parks in No-Honor States
Parks in California, New York, and other no-honor states follow those restrictive state laws. Even though the park is federal property, the state's firearms laws govern carry within the park. If you can't carry in CA, you can't carry in Yosemite.

Good-to-Know: Always Check

  • Each park may have additional local regulations posted
  • Some tribal lands within park boundaries have separate rules
  • Wilderness areas within national forests: generally follow state law
  • Park concession facilities (lodges, restaurants): check if they're on federal property
  • Campground camp stores: usually federal — leave firearm secured in vehicle

Best Practices for Park Carry

  • Check which state(s) the park is in before arrival
  • Verify your permit is honored in that state
  • Keep firearm secured when visiting any facility building
  • Carry your permit card at all times
  • Wildlife encounters: know when/if brandishing is legally justified
  • Bears vs. firearms: bear spray is statistically more effective but carry both if legal
