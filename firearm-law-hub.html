Magazine Capacity Laws

14 states plus D.C. restrict magazine capacity. 36 states have no limit. Oregon, Washington, and DC are all under active litigation as of early 2026.

What to Check Before Buying

  • Know your magazine's base capacity — round count with the stock baseplate installed
  • Know your state's limit — verify against current state statutes, not just this table
  • Check local ordinances — some cities are stricter than their state (Boulder, CO: 10-round limit)
  • Grandfathered magazines — adding capacity may void grandfathered status in some states
  • When in doubt, consult an attorney — magazine law is complex and heavily litigated

National Firearms Act

Suppressor Laws by State

42 states allow civilian suppressor ownership with NFA compliance. 8 states plus D.C. ban ownership entirely. The $200 tax stamp was eliminated January 1, 2026.

✓ 2026 Update — $0 Tax Stamp: The $200 NFA transfer tax on suppressors was eliminated effective January 1, 2026. The ATF Form 4 approval process and background check remain fully in place. You still need ATF approval before taking possession. State-level bans are completely unaffected by this federal change.
✖
Banned — 9 Jurisdictions
California, Hawaii, Illinois, Maryland, Massachusetts, New Jersey, New York, Rhode Island, and D.C. prohibit civilian suppressor ownership. Federal NFA compliance does not override these bans. Felony charges apply in most cases.
✓
Legal — 42 States
Civilian ownership allowed with NFA compliance. File ATF eForm 4, pass background check, register with ATF. $200 tax stamp eliminated as of January 2026. eForm 4 approvals now averaging days for most individual and trust filers.
⚠
Restricted — 3 States
Connecticut & Pennsylvania — ownership legal, hunting use prohibited. Minnesota — ownership legal but requires a local law enforcement transfer permit AND bans hunting use. Always verify state hunting regs separately from ownership rules.
Federal NFA Process
1
Purchase From a Licensed NFA Dealer

Find a dealer who handles NFA transfers. The suppressor stays at the dealer until your Form 4 is approved — you cannot take possession beforehand.

2
Submit ATF eForm 4

File electronically at eForms.atf.gov. Choose individual or trust. Trust allows multiple designated trustees to legally use the suppressor and simplifies estate transfer — no second Form 4 when passing to beneficiaries.

Individual FilingFastest approvals. Near real-time in some cases. No sharing the suppressor with others legally.
Trust FilingMultiple trustees can legally use it. Simplifies estate planning. ~3 days average approval currently.
3
ATF Background Check & Approval

ATF conducts a background check and reviews the application. Once approved, they return your Form 4 with a stamp. As of early 2026 eForm 4 wait times are dramatically reduced — days, not the months or years of previous years.

Tax stamp fee: $0 as of January 1, 2026 (previously $200)

4
Take Possession — Keep Your Form 4

Pick up your suppressor from the dealer with your approved Form 4. Keep the documentation with the suppressor at all times. When traveling interstate: suppressors and AOWs do not require ATF Form 5320.20. SBRs, SBSs, and machine guns do.

⛔ Travel Warning: Driving through a ban state with a suppressor is illegal even with a valid Form 4. New Jersey and New York have arrested travelers despite apparent FOPA compliance. Never transit through ban states with a suppressor. When flying: declare as a firearm at check-in.

Interstate Transport

Traveling With Firearms

FOPA provides some interstate travel protections — but they don't cover accessories, magazines, or extended stops. Know the laws of every state on your route before you leave.

FOPA Safe Passage — What It Covers

  • Unloaded firearm — must be unloaded during transport at all times
  • Locked hard-sided container — not in glove box or center console
  • Direct travel — from a state where legal to a state where legal
  • Not extended stays — stopping for an extended period may void protection

What FOPA Does NOT Cover

  • Magazine capacity violations — a 15-round magazine through NJ is still illegal
  • Suppressor transport through ban states — never transit ban states with a suppressor
  • NJ and NY have arrested FOPA-compliant travelers — avoid entirely when possible
  • Airport layovers in restrictive states — your checked firearm is still in that jurisdiction
  • Accessories with state-specific restrictions (suppressors, SBRs)
Air Travel
✈
TSA Rules
Unloaded, in a locked hard-sided container, declared at check-in. Never in a carry-on. Penalties are federal and severe.
💵
Ammunition
Must be in original factory packaging or a container designed for that caliber. Loose rounds are not compliant. Check airline-specific limits.
⚠
Destination Laws
Flying into a restricted state means your mags must comply upon arrival. Flying into NJ with 15-round mags is illegal regardless of origin.
