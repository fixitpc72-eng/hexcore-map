Your Ownership
Plan

Responsible gun ownership includes planning for the full picture — not just the threat coming through the door, but also what happens if life gets hard. The best time to make this plan is now, when you're in a good headspace. Fill it out, save it, and never need it. That's the goal.

The premise
Owning a firearm is a right and a responsibility. Planning ahead is what separates a responsible owner from everyone else. This tool helps you think through situations before they happen.
Your rights, intact
Voluntarily seeking help does not trigger a firearms prohibition. Only an involuntary commitment under specific legal criteria affects gun rights. Getting help is not losing your guns.
This is not a lecture
This tool doesn't assume anything about you. It's a planning resource — same as a home defense plan or a will. Responsible people plan. That's all this is.

01
Trusted Contact Plan
Someone who knows your setup, knows where your firearms are, and has a plan if something happens to you — or if you ever ask them to hold your firearms temporarily.
👤
What a trusted contact does
▼

A trusted contact is someone you've specifically told: where your firearms are, how they're stored, what your wishes are if something happens to you, and that they have permission to temporarily hold your firearms at your request.

This is not about them having access to your firearms without your knowledge. It's about having a named, prepared person who knows your plan and can act on it if you ask — whether that's during a rough patch, a hospital stay, a deployment, or after your death.

A trusted contact should be an adult you trust completely, who is legally eligible to possess firearms, and who is willing to have an honest conversation about this role in advance. A spouse, sibling, close friend, or fellow gun owner all work. Choose someone who won't panic and who understands firearms.

💬
Having the conversation
▼

This conversation doesn't have to be heavy. It can be as simple as: "I want to make sure someone I trust knows where everything is and what to do if I'm ever in the hospital or going through a rough time. Would you be willing to be that person?"

Tell them: where your firearms are stored, combination or key location (in a secure way — written down, not memorized by them), what your wishes are for your firearms if you pass away, and that you may someday ask them to hold your firearms temporarily. Make sure they're comfortable with that role.

Have this conversation once, update it if your situation changes. That's all it takes.

02
Temporary Storage Plan
Situations where you might want your firearms out of the house temporarily — and where they can go. Plan this now so the option is ready if you ever need it.
📋
When temporary storage makes sense
▼

Temporary storage is a completely normal part of responsible ownership. Common situations where gun owners choose to temporarily move their firearms out of the home:

A household member is going through a mental health crisis or rough patch
You're going through a difficult period and want to put distance between yourself and your firearms — on your own initiative
You have a guest staying who should not have access (court order, substance issues, age)
Extended travel, deployment, or hospital stay
Home renovation with contractors in and out
Divorce proceedings or elevated household conflict

Temporary storage is voluntary, reversible, and does not affect your rights. It's the same decision as putting your firearms in a storage unit when you move. You get them back when you're ready.

🏠
Where your firearms can go
▼
👤
Trusted adult outside household
Someone you trust who is legally eligible to possess firearms and has a secure place to store them. This is the most private option — no paperwork, no record.
Most Private
🏪
Licensed FFL dealer / gun shop
Many gun shops will store firearms temporarily for a small fee. Call ahead — not all offer this. The shop holds your firearms as a consignment or storage item. You retrieve them when you're ready.
Call Ahead
🚔
Law enforcement
Some police departments accept voluntary temporary storage. Call your local non-emergency line first — do not walk into a station with firearms unannounced. Ask about their process and how to retrieve.
Call First
Legal note: Temporary transfers to trusted adults may be considered a legal transfer in some states and could require a background check or FFL involvement. Check your state's private transfer laws. An FFL dealer can always facilitate a legal transfer. When in doubt, use a dealer.

03
Off-Body Checklist
What to do before your firearms leave your possession — whether temporarily, for travel, or permanently. Run this list every time.
✓
Before firearms leave your possession
▼

Run this checklist any time your firearms are leaving your home — temporary storage, travel, lending, or estate transfer.

Unloaded and verified clear — visually and physically confirm every chamber is empty before transport
Ammunition stored separately — in a different bag or container from the firearm
Trigger lock or case lock applied — even if going to a trusted adult, lock it
Serial numbers documented — photographed or written down in a secure location
Person receiving it is legally eligible — not prohibited from possessing firearms
Understand your state's transfer law — some states require FFL involvement even for temporary transfers
Retrieval plan is clear — both you and the holder know exactly how and when you get them back
NFA items: Form 5320.20 if crossing state lines — SBRs, suppressors, machine guns require ATF notification for interstate transport
Approved Form 4 or Form 1 is going with the NFA item — or a copy is held by the person receiving it
Your trusted contact knows — the person listed in Section 01 is aware the firearms are now elsewhere
✓ Good habit: Keep trigger locks and a lockable soft case accessible — not buried in the safe. If your plan is to quickly move firearms to a trusted person's home in an emergency, friction in that process costs time. Make it easy.
📄
What documentation travels with them
▼

Keep a folder — physical or digital — with these documents. Give a copy to whoever is holding your firearms.

Approved NFA Form 4 or Form 1 (for suppressors, SBRs, SBSs, AOWs, machine guns)
Serial number record — photograph of the firearm's serial number location
Your contact information — so the holder can reach you
Retrieval agreement — simple written note stating the terms of temporary storage
ATF Form 5320.20 (if NFA items are crossing state lines)
Legal note: A simple written note between you and your storage contact is not a legal document but creates a clear record of intent. For permanent transfers, use an FFL. For NFA items, always involve an FFL. This tool is not legal advice — consult an attorney if you're unsure about your state's transfer laws.
04
If Today Is Hard
This section is for you — or for someone you're worried about. No judgment. No lecture. Just information.
● Read this first

If you're reading this section because you're having a hard day — that takes honesty, and honesty takes strength. Most crises are temporary. Most rough patches pass. The decisions you make in a low moment can be permanent. That's the only reason this section exists.

You built this plan earlier when you were in a good headspace for exactly this moment. Check Section 02 — your temporary storage plan is already filled out. You know where they can go. You know who to call. The hard part of the planning is done.

▸ What to do right now
1
Put distance between yourself and your firearms. You don't have to give them up permanently. Just move them somewhere else for now — Section 02 has your plan. Call your trusted contact from Section 01.
2
Call someone you trust. Not to explain everything — just to not be alone. The name in Section 01 is a starting point. A friend, family member, pastor, or fellow veteran. Anyone.
3
If you need more support than a conversation, call 988. It is free, confidential, available 24/7, and staffed by trained people. You do not have to be in immediate danger to call. If today is just hard — that qualifies.
4
Remember: voluntary help does not cost you your firearms rights. Seeking support is not the same as involuntary commitment. You are not losing your guns by asking for help.

❤
If you're worried about someone else
▼

If someone in your household is going through a hard time and you're concerned about access to firearms — you can act. You don't need to wait for a crisis to become an emergency.

Have a direct conversation. Ask plainly: "Are you having thoughts of hurting yourself?" Research consistently shows that asking does not plant the idea — it opens a door. If they say yes, ask them to let you hold their firearms temporarily. Frame it as care, not control.

Offer to help move the firearms, not demand it. "Can I hold these for you for a while?" is more effective than ultimatums. They come back when the person is ready. This is a plan, not a permanent removal.

If you believe they are in immediate danger, call 911. That is the right call. No planning tool replaces emergency services when someone is in immediate danger to themselves or others.

The 988 Lifeline also has resources for people helping a loved one — you don't have to be in crisis yourself to call.
